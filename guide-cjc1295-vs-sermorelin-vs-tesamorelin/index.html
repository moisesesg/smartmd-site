CJC-1295 vs Sermorelin vs Tesamorelin

Three GHRH analogues with different half-lives and research profiles.

Sermorelin, CJC-1295, and Tesamorelin are synthetic analogues of growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH). They share a common mechanism — GHRH receptor activation — but differ meaningfully in half-life, modification strategy, and research literature depth. Here is the comparison.

DIRECT ANSWER

All three activate the GHRH receptor to stimulate pulsatile GH release from the anterior pituitary. Sermorelin is the shortest (29 amino acids, minutes of half-life). CJC-1295 extends half-life via DAC (Drug Affinity Complex) conjugation — with DAC: days; without DAC: ~30 minutes. Tesamorelin is the only GHRH analogue that received FDA approval in pharmaceutical form for a specific indication — the research-grade version has an extensive preclinical literature on visceral adiposity and body composition.

Sermorelin — the pulsatile baseline

Sermorelin is the first 29 amino acids of endogenous GHRH — the minimum fragment needed to activate the GHRH receptor. Its very short half-life (~10 minutes in preclinical models) means it produces a single pulse of GH release that rapidly clears. This makes it valuable for researching pulsatile GH dynamics without introducing sustained elevation.

Why researchers choose Sermorelin

  • Preserves natural pulsatile GH rhythm in preclinical models
  • Short half-life allows clean temporal resolution in pharmacokinetic studies
  • Large historical literature base — one of the most-studied GHRH analogues
  • Useful as a positive control when evaluating other GHRH analogues

CJC-1295 — the extended half-life variant

CJC-1295 exists in two forms: with DAC (Drug Affinity Complex) conjugation and without. With-DAC CJC-1295 covalently binds albumin via a maleimide linker, producing an extended preclinical half-life of approximately 6-8 days. Without DAC, CJC-1295 has a half-life similar to modified GHRH (~30 minutes). The no-DAC variant is often called "Mod GRF 1-29."

CJC-1295 with DAC

Extended half-life (~days). Produces sustained GH elevation rather than a pulsatile pattern. Used in research contexts where chronic GH axis elevation is the study question.

CJC-1295 no-DAC (Mod GRF 1-29)

Short half-life (~30 min). Preserves pulsatile GH release. Commonly stacked with Ipamorelin (selective GHSR agonist) in research protocols studying combined GHRH + GHS pathway activation.

Tesamorelin — the FDA-pathway GHRH

Tesamorelin is a 44-amino-acid GHRH analogue modified with a trans-3-hexenoic acid group at the N-terminus to enhance stability. It is the only GHRH analogue to have received FDA approval in pharmaceutical form for a specific indication (HIV-associated lipodystrophy). The research-grade version has an extensive preclinical literature base specifically in visceral adiposity and body composition models.

Why Tesamorelin stands out

  • 40+ peer-reviewed preclinical studies on visceral adiposity specifically
  • More than any other GHRH analogue studied for trunk fat reduction in preclinical models
  • Well-characterized pharmacokinetic profile from the pharmaceutical development pathway
  • Often combined with Ipamorelin for GHRH + GHS dual pathway research — see 2X Blend Tesamorelin + Ipamorelin

Which GHRH to choose for your research

Pulsatile GH research

Choose Sermorelin or CJC-1295 no-DAC. Both preserve natural pulsatile GH rhythm. Sermorelin has deeper historical literature; no-DAC CJC-1295 is the more common modern choice in stacks.

Sustained GH elevation research

Choose CJC-1295 with DAC. Its albumin-bound extended half-life produces days of elevated GH signal — useful for studying chronic GH axis effects in research models.

Visceral adiposity / body composition

Choose Tesamorelin. It has the deepest preclinical literature on visceral fat reduction and body composition specifically. Commonly paired with Ipamorelin in blended research protocols.

Replicating specific published protocols

Consult the primary literature. Older studies more often used Sermorelin; modern stacks more often use CJC-1295 no-DAC + Ipamorelin; adiposity-focused work tends toward Tesamorelin.

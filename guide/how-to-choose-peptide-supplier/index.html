RESEARCH GUIDE

How to Choose a Peptide Supplier

The diligence checklist that separates research-grade suppliers from the rest.

The peptide supplier market has a wide quality distribution. Good suppliers produce 99%+ pure material with lot-specific documentation. Bad suppliers ship unverified material with generic marketing COAs. This guide walks through how to tell them apart.

IMMEDIATE RED FLAGS

If you see any of these, consider the supplier unverified:

  • No lot-specific COAs — only a generic "product COA" marketing document
  • Missing mass spectrometry on the COA (purity without identity is meaningless)
  • Missing endotoxin testing on the COA
  • Only one lab doing all testing (and probably owned by the supplier's manufacturer)
  • Labeled for "human use" or with dosing instructions
  • Therapeutic claims ("treats X", "cures Y") — regulatory and quality red flag
  • No physical U.S. address, no phone number, no LLC registration visible
  • Payment only via crypto with no invoice trail
  • Unusually low prices for named compounds (GLP-1 peptides, GLP-3 RT, Reta-class compounds)

Green signals — what to look for

Quality signals

  • Lot-specific COAs publicly accessible before purchase
  • HPLC + mass spec + endotoxin on every COA
  • Multiple independent analytical labs (not a single captive lab)
  • 99%+ purity as a stated, documented standard
  • U.S.-based testing labs preferred (ISO 17025 accreditation is a bonus)

Business signals

  • Registered U.S. business with physical address
  • Phone number, email, real customer service
  • Clear Research Use Only labeling
  • No therapeutic claims anywhere in marketing
  • Published founder / team information — accountability trail

Operational signals

  • Real order tracking, real shipping, real inventory
  • Clear refund / replacement policy
  • Responsive to pre-purchase questions with specific answers
  • Willing to share specific lot documentation before purchase

Compliance signals

  • Wholesale/institutional tier with research-context verification
  • Transparent on what is and isn't available
  • References regulatory framework correctly
  • Doesn't promise human outcomes on research compounds

The pricing paradox

Peptides are not a commodity. Two bottles labeled "BPC-157 10mg" from different suppliers can differ in purity from 95% to 99.5%, in identity confirmation, in endotoxin load, and in actual fill weight. A 30% price difference can reflect meaningfully different underlying material.

However, the reverse is also true: highest price does not guarantee highest quality. Some suppliers charge premium prices without doing the extra testing that would justify them. The COA is the truth-teller, not the price tag.

The one-minute supplier test: Ask any supplier to send you a lot-specific COA for a product they currently have in stock, before you place an order. A quality supplier will send it in minutes. A marketing operation will deflect, stall, or send a generic document. This single test separates the two categories reliably.

What is the Wolverine Blend?

The Wolverine Blend is a synergistic combination of the two most-studied recovery peptides in preclinical research: BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157, 5mg) and TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4 fragment, 5mg) — combined in a single lyophilized vial. With 150+ combined preclinical references, this blend represents the most extensively studied peptide pairing for musculoskeletal recovery models.

BPC-157 is a 15-amino-acid peptide fragment originally isolated from gastric juice, studied for tissue repair signaling and angiogenesis regulation. TB-500 is a synthetic fragment of Thymosin Beta-4, studied for its role in cell migration, actin regulation, and systemic tissue repair signaling. Together, researchers examine their complementary and potentially synergistic mechanisms.

The Wolverine Blend is classified as a research-use-only compound. It is not FDA-approved for any therapeutic indication. All references below describe preclinical (in vitro or animal model) research contexts, not human clinical outcomes.

Published preclinical research areas

Combined tissue signaling

Research examines the synergistic effects of BPC-157 and TB-500 in tissue repair models — exploring complementary signaling pathways including VEGF activation, actin polymerization, and fibroblast migration in musculoskeletal recovery contexts.

Synergistic musculoskeletal recovery

Preclinical studies examine the pairing of BPC-157's tendon/ligament signaling with TB-500's systemic repair cascade in models of joint recovery, muscle tissue repair, and connective tissue regeneration.

Angiogenesis

Both peptides independently demonstrate effects on new blood vessel formation in preclinical models. Combined research explores potentially amplified vascularization signaling through complementary VEGF and NO pathway interactions.

Inflammation modulation

Published research examines both peptides' effects on inflammatory marker modulation — BPC-157 through GI-cytoprotective pathways and TB-500 through actin-sequestering anti-inflammatory mechanisms in tissue injury models.

What is Kisspeptin?

Kisspeptin is a neuropeptide encoded by the KISS1 gene, originally discovered in 1996 at the Pennsylvania State University College of Medicine. It was initially identified as a metastasis suppressor, but its role in reproductive endocrinology — specifically as the master regulator of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis — has become its primary research focus.

Kisspeptin acts on the GPR54 (KISS1R) receptor on GnRH neurons in the hypothalamus, triggering gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) secretion. This single signaling event initiates the entire downstream cascade of LH and FSH release from the pituitary, making kisspeptin the upstream "master switch" of reproductive hormone regulation. Research has examined its roles in puberty onset, fertility, and HPG axis modulation.

Kisspeptin is classified as a research-use-only compound. It is not FDA-approved for any therapeutic indication. All references below describe preclinical (in vitro or animal model) research contexts, not human clinical outcomes.

Published preclinical research areas

GnRH neuron activation

Kisspeptin is the most potent known stimulator of GnRH secretion. It binds to KISS1R on GnRH neurons in the hypothalamus, triggering pulsatile GnRH release. Research examines its signaling kinetics, receptor desensitization patterns, and dose-response relationships.

Reproductive endocrinology

As the upstream regulator of the entire HPG axis, kisspeptin research spans LH/FSH regulation, ovulation models, spermatogenesis, and sex steroid feedback loops. It has become central to understanding the neuroendocrine control of reproduction.

HPG axis regulation

Preclinical studies explore kisspeptin's role in modulating the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis — including its interactions with neurokinin B and dynorphin in the "KNDy neuron" model, which is the current paradigm for understanding GnRH pulse generation.

Puberty onset & fertility research

Loss-of-function mutations in KISS1 or KISS1R cause hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and absent puberty. Research explores kisspeptin's role in puberty timing, seasonal breeding regulation, and potential applications in fertility protocol research.

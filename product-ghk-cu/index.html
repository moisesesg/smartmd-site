What is GHK-Cu?

GHK-Cu (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine copper complex) is a naturally occurring tripeptide-copper complex found in human plasma, saliva, and urine. First identified in 1973, it has become one of the most extensively studied peptides in skin biology, wound healing, and tissue remodeling research — with over 75 peer-reviewed publications.

Plasma levels of GHK-Cu decline significantly with age — from approximately 200 ng/mL at age 20 to 80 ng/mL by age 60. This age-related decline has made it a primary subject of longevity and regenerative research. The copper ion is essential for its biological activity, facilitating interactions with collagen, decorin, and multiple growth factor pathways.

GHK-Cu is classified as a research-use-only compound in injectable form. It is not FDA-approved for any therapeutic indication. All references below describe preclinical (in vitro or animal model) research contexts, not human clinical outcomes.

Published preclinical research areas

Collagen synthesis signaling

Extensively studied for its role in stimulating collagen I, III, and elastin production in dermal fibroblast models. Research demonstrates GHK-Cu's ability to upregulate extracellular matrix protein synthesis and tissue remodeling genes.

Wound healing

Multiple publications examine GHK-Cu's effects on wound contraction, angiogenesis, and inflammatory modulation in tissue repair models. Research shows accelerated wound closure and improved tissue architecture in preclinical wound models.

Longevity skin models

Studied for its effects on skin thickness, elasticity, and photoaging markers. The age-related decline of endogenous GHK-Cu makes it particularly relevant to chronological and UV-induced skin aging research.

Hair follicle regeneration

Preclinical research examines GHK-Cu's effects on hair follicle size, dermal papilla cell proliferation, and the transition from telogen to anagen phase in hair cycle models.

GHK-Cu is studied extensively in dermal fibroblast and skin aging models. Research examines its ability to stimulate collagen I, III, and elastin synthesis, improve skin thickness and elasticity markers, and modulate genes associated with photoaging and chronological aging.

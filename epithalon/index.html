What is Epithalon?

Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon) is a synthetic tetrapeptide with the sequence Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly. It was originally developed by Professor Vladimir Khavinson at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology in the 1990s as a synthetic analog of the naturally occurring polypeptide epithalamin, which is produced by the pineal gland.

The primary research interest in Epithalon centers on its role in telomerase activation — the enzyme responsible for maintaining telomere length at the ends of chromosomes. Preclinical studies have examined its effects on telomere elongation models, pineal gland function, melatonin production regulation, and broader aging research. Its low molecular weight and simple peptide structure make it highly stable and well-suited for laboratory research.

Epithalon is classified as a research-use-only compound. It is not FDA-approved for any therapeutic indication. All references below describe preclinical (in vitro or animal model) research contexts, not human clinical outcomes.

Published preclinical research areas

Telomerase activation

The most-studied property of Epithalon. Preclinical models have examined its ability to activate telomerase, the enzyme that adds telomeric repeats to chromosome ends. Research suggests potential effects on cellular replicative lifespan in vitro.

Telomere elongation models

Linked to telomerase activation, studies have explored whether Epithalon administration in preclinical models correlates with measurable changes in telomere length — a key biomarker in aging and cellular senescence research.

Pineal gland function

As a synthetic analog of epithalamin, Epithalon has been studied for its effects on pineal gland bioregulation, including potential modulation of melatonin synthesis and circadian rhythm signaling in aging animal models.

Aging research

Broader longevity studies in preclinical models have explored Epithalon's effects on lifespan extension, antioxidant enzyme activity, and age-related changes in gene expression patterns across multiple organ systems.

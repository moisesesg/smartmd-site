What is BPC-157?

BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound 157) is a 15-amino-acid peptide fragment originally isolated from human gastric juice. It is one of the most extensively studied research peptides in preclinical literature, with over 100 peer-reviewed publications examining its role in musculoskeletal tissue signaling, angiogenesis regulation, gut epithelium support, and tendon-to-bone healing models.

Unlike many research peptides that degrade rapidly in acidic environments, BPC-157 is notably stable in gastric acid — a property that has made it particularly interesting in GI-related preclinical research. Its molecular weight is approximately 1,419 Da.

BPC-157 is classified as a research-use-only compound. It is not FDA-approved for any therapeutic indication. All references below describe preclinical (in vitro or animal model) research contexts, not human clinical outcomes.

Published preclinical research areas

Musculoskeletal tissue signaling

Studied in preclinical models of tendon healing, ligament repair, muscle tissue recovery, and bone-tendon junction regeneration. Multiple publications examine its effects on collagen organization and fibroblast migration in tissue repair models.

Angiogenesis regulation

Preclinical studies examine BPC-157's role in new blood vessel formation and vascular repair signaling. Research has explored its interactions with the VEGF pathway and nitric oxide system in tissue vascularization models.

Gut epithelium support

Originally derived from gastric juice, BPC-157 has been studied extensively in preclinical GI models — including gastric ulcer, intestinal damage, and inflammatory bowel models. Its stability in acidic environments is a distinguishing characteristic.

Neuroprotective signaling

Emerging preclinical literature examines BPC-157 in models of dopaminergic and serotonergic pathway modulation, peripheral nerve repair, and CNS injury recovery signaling.

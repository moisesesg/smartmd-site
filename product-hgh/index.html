What is HGH?

Human Growth Hormone (HGH), also known as somatotropin, is a 191-amino-acid single-chain polypeptide produced by somatotropic cells of the anterior pituitary gland. It is the most extensively studied hormone in endocrinology, with over 500 peer-reviewed publications spanning more than five decades of research.

Recombinant HGH (rHGH) is produced using recombinant DNA technology to create a bioidentical 191-amino-acid protein that is structurally identical to endogenous human growth hormone. Research applications focus on growth hormone pathway signaling, body composition models, bone density studies, and cell regeneration mechanisms. HGH acts both directly on target tissues and indirectly through stimulation of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) production in the liver.

HGH is classified as a research-use-only compound in this context. All references below describe research contexts. HGH is a controlled substance in some jurisdictions — researchers should verify local regulations before acquisition.

Published research areas

Growth hormone pathway research

HGH is the foundational compound for studying the GH/IGF-1 axis — one of the most important signaling pathways in endocrinology. Research examines receptor binding, signal transduction, and downstream effects on gene expression and protein synthesis.

Body composition models

Extensive research examines HGH's effects on lean mass, adipose tissue distribution, and metabolic rate. Studies explore both the direct lipolytic effects and indirect anabolic signaling through IGF-1 in various preclinical and clinical research contexts.

Bone density

HGH plays a critical role in bone metabolism research. Studies examine its effects on osteoblast activity, calcium absorption, bone mineral density, and skeletal growth — particularly relevant in growth hormone deficiency models and aging research.

Cell regeneration

Research explores HGH's role in cellular repair and regeneration, including wound healing models, tissue repair, and organ regeneration studies. Its effects on cell proliferation and differentiation are central to regenerative medicine research.
